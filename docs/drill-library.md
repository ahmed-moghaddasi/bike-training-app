# Apex Lab Drill Library

This file defines the first Apex Lab drill library. It is app content, not final UI copy. The app should later turn each section into concise cards, diagrams, setup instructions, camera guidance, and coaching prompts.

## Research Basis

The library combines several training perspectives:

- Road-racing fundamentals: vision, line, radius control, brake release, body timing, and throttle timing.
- Spanish-style pitbike/minimoto training: small bikes, cones, repetition, video review, and precise references.
- Pitbike/minimoto parking-lot practice: compact layouts, low-speed repetition, safe progression, and consistency over hero laps.
- App content strategy: each drill needs setup, camera placement, timing rules, mistakes, progressions, and review prompts.

## Library Principles

- Start with technique, not speed.
- Use a closed, clean, paved practice area.
- Wear full protective gear.
- Warm tires and brakes before timed laps.
- Move only one variable at a time.
- Log times only within the same bike, drill, and setup variant.
- If a progression changes the measured path, it becomes a new setup variant.
- Best lap matters, but consistency matters more for training.
- Video review should focus on one cue at a time.

## App-Wide Safety Gate

Every drill should show a short safety reminder before the rider starts:

- Closed lot only.
- Full gear.
- Clean surface, no gravel or oil.
- Leave runoff around the layout.
- Stop if traffic, pedestrians, fatigue, or mechanical issues appear.
- Begin slow enough that mistakes are boring.

# 1. Circle

## Summary

Circle is the foundation drill. It teaches the rider to hold a constant radius, look through a corner, relax the bars, keep throttle steady, and build comfort with lean without the workload of a full course.

## What This Trains

- Constant-radius cornering.
- Eyes up and through the turn.
- Relaxed arms and light hands.
- Stable throttle.
- Body position held before and during the corner.
- Feeling how speed changes corner radius.

## Why It Matters On Track

Every track corner asks the rider to control radius. If the rider cannot hold one clean circle, they will struggle to hit an apex, stay on line, or open throttle predictably on a real corner.

## Default Setup Variant

Name: `8m circle`

Recommended for pitbike/minimoto or small lightweight bike.

Measurements:

- Circle diameter: 8m.
- Radius: 4m.
- Cone count: 8 perimeter cones plus 1 center cone.
- Space needed: roughly 12m x 12m minimum.

Notes:

- Use chalk/string/tape measure to mark the radius.
- If using a full-size motorcycle, start larger: 10m to 12m diameter.
- The 8m circle should be treated as a different setup variant from a 10m or 12m circle.

## Setup Variants

- `8m circle`: default pitbike/minimoto setup.
- `10m circle`: easier or full-size bike setup.
- `6m circle`: advanced tight setup; separate timing context.

## Cone Placement

1. Mark the center point.
2. Measure 4m from center and place the first perimeter cone.
3. Place 7 more cones evenly around the circle.
4. Keep the center cone visible for setup, then remove it or leave it low/flat if safe.
5. Mark a timing line across one point of the circle.

## Camera Placement

Position:

- Tripod 6m to 10m outside the circle, aimed toward the timing line.
- Height: waist to chest height.

What the camera should see:

- Full circle if possible.
- Timing line clearly visible.
- Rider entering and leaving the timing zone.

Timing point:

- One fixed line on the circle, ideally nearest the camera.

Detection zone:

- A vertical or diagonal line through the timing marker.

## Timing Rule

- Timer starts on the first detected pass through the timing line.
- Each later pass through the same line is one lap.
- Clockwise and counterclockwise should be separate session notes, and may become separate setup variants later if needed.

## How To Ride It

1. Enter the circle slowly and settle into a clean radius.
2. Set body position before the timing line, not halfway around the circle.
3. Keep eyes level and looking around the circle, not at the next cone.
4. Hold steady maintenance throttle.
5. Keep hands soft; support yourself with legs and core.
6. Exit only after finishing the lap or session.

## Coaching Cues

- "One radius. One throttle. One clean line."
- "Eyes lead. Hands only follow."
- "Do not fix the line with panic throttle."
- "If your arms are tight, your timing is late."
- "Same circle, same sound, same body."

## Common Mistakes

- Looking down at the cones.
- Chopping throttle mid-circle.
- Adding throttle to save a wide line.
- Moving body position after the bike is already leaned.
- Inside arm locked.
- Circle slowly grows wider each lap.

## Video Review Prompts

- Is the rider looking through the circle or down at the ground?
- Does the line stay the same size every lap?
- Does the engine note stay steady?
- Is body position set before lean?
- Are arms relaxed or braced?

## Success Metrics

- No foot dabs.
- No cone touches.
- Lap times within 5 percent across 5 laps.
- Similar line every lap.
- Smooth engine sound with no repeated throttle chop.

## Progressions

Same timing context:

- Run 5 laps with no foot dabs.
- Run 5 laps with lap spread under 5 percent.
- Repeat both directions.
- Add a focus cue: relaxed inside elbow, eyes farther around, lighter grip.

New setup variant:

- Shrink from 10m to 8m diameter.
- Shrink from 8m to 6m diameter.
- Add an apex marker and require throttle pickup only after that point.

# 2. Figure Eight

## Summary

Figure Eight trains the transition from one side of the bike to the other. It is not just a tight-turn drill. For track riding, the value is body timing through the crossover and being ready before the next corner begins.

## What This Trains

- Left-right body transition.
- Vision reset from one corner to the next.
- Countersteer timing.
- Throttle timing through exit and transition.
- Setting body position before corner entry.
- Repeating a line without drifting.

## Why It Matters On Track

Chicanes, linked corners, and track direction changes punish late body movement. A rider who changes body position after turn-in is already behind. Figure Eight teaches the body to lead the bike.

## Default Setup Variant

Name: `8m double circle`

Measurements:

- Two circles, each 8m diameter.
- Radius: 4m each.
- Circle centers: 8m apart.
- Crossover point: where the two circles touch.
- Cone count: 9 minimum.
- Space needed: roughly 18m x 10m minimum.

Cone count:

- 4 perimeter cones for left circle.
- 4 perimeter cones for right circle.
- 1 crossover cone.

Notes:

- PSRA describes large figure eights with 4m-radius circles and a 10m x 20m or larger area. Apex Lab adapts that into a simple 9-cone layout for the app.
- Full-size bikes should start larger: 10m to 12m circles.

## Setup Variants

- `8m double circle`: default pitbike/minimoto setup.
- `10m double circle`: easier or full-size bike setup.
- `6m double circle`: advanced tight setup; separate timing context.

## Cone Placement

1. Mark the first circle center.
2. Measure 8m across and mark the second circle center.
3. Place 4 cones around the first circle at 4m radius.
4. Place 4 cones around the second circle at 4m radius.
5. Place 1 cone at the crossover where the circles touch.
6. Mark the timing line through the crossover.

## Camera Placement

Position:

- Tripod near the crossover cone, offset 3m to 5m outside the riding path.
- Height: waist to chest height.

What the camera should see:

- Both circles.
- The crossover.
- The rider's body transition before each circle.

Timing point:

- Crossover line.

Detection zone:

- A line through the crossover cone.

## Timing Rule

- Timer starts on the first pass through the crossover.
- One lap is one full figure-eight cycle returning to the same crossover direction.
- The app may also count every crossover pass as a split later, but v1 should time full cycles.

## How To Ride It

1. Enter the first circle on the outer path.
2. Look across and through the circle, not at the crossover cone.
3. Begin body transition before the bike reaches the crossover.
4. Cross the timing line with the body already moving to the next side.
5. Settle into the next circle before turn-in.
6. Add throttle only when the bike has direction.

## Coaching Cues

- "The body moves before the bike asks for it."
- "Do not arrive at the crossover neutral and surprised."
- "Eyes to the next exit before the front wheel gets there."
- "Transition clean. Then corner."
- "Fast body. Calm hands."

## Common Mistakes

- Rider crosses the center still looking at the old circle.
- Body moves after turn-in.
- Bike cuts inside the circle.
- Throttle blips at random points.
- Rider rushes the crossover and loses radius.
- One direction is clean, the other is late and stiff.

## Video Review Prompts

- Does the rider's head move toward the next circle before the bike transitions?
- Is the body set before entering each circle?
- Are both circles the same size?
- Is the throttle clean through exit/transition?
- Does the rider look through the next circle or at the crossover cone?

## Success Metrics

- No foot dabs.
- No cone touches.
- Same path through both circles.
- 5 clean laps with lap spread under 5 percent.
- Body transition visibly starts before the crossover.

## Progressions

Same timing context:

- 5 clean laps clockwise starting direction.
- 5 clean laps opposite starting direction.
- Count only laps where body is set before entry.
- Add a review score for eyes, body timing, line, throttle.

New setup variant:

- Shrink circles from 10m to 8m.
- Shrink circles from 8m to 6m.
- Add a tighter crossover gate.

# 3. Hairpin

## Summary

Hairpin trains the slowest, tightest corner: brake, turn, wait, point the bike, then drive out. It is where early turn-in and early throttle are most obvious.

## What This Trains

- Braking marker discipline.
- Slowest-point control.
- Tight apex precision.
- Late direction and exit patience.
- Throttle pickup after direction.
- Keeping the bike from running wide.

## Why It Matters On Track

Hairpins punish impatience. If the rider turns too early or opens throttle before the bike is pointed, the exit disappears. This drill builds the habit of finishing the corner before asking the bike to accelerate.

## Default Setup Variant

Name: `standard hairpin`

Measurements:

- Approach straight: 18m to 25m.
- Brake cone: 12m to 18m before turn-in cone.
- Turn-in cone: end of approach straight.
- Apex cone: 4m inside the turn-in line.
- Exit gate: 4m to 6m after the apex, aligned with exit straight.
- Exit lane width: 3m to 4m.
- Cone count: 5 to 6.
- Space needed: roughly 25m x 14m.

Notes:

- Start slower and wider if using a full-size bike.
- Do not turn this into a braking-late contest.

## Setup Variants

- `standard hairpin`: default.
- `wide hairpin`: larger radius/easier entry.
- `tight hairpin`: apex moved inward; separate timing context.
- `late-apex hairpin`: apex moved later; separate timing context if timed.

## Cone Placement

1. Mark a straight approach lane.
2. Place the brake cone on the approach.
3. Place the turn-in cone at the end of the straight.
4. Place the apex cone inside the turn, roughly 4m from turn-in.
5. Place two exit gate cones on the exit line.
6. Optional: place an outside track-limit cone to punish running wide.

## Camera Placement

Position:

- Tripod outside the apex, angled back toward brake cone and forward to exit.
- Height: waist to chest height.

What the camera should see:

- Brake cone.
- Turn-in point.
- Apex.
- Exit gate.
- Rider body and bike direction at apex.

Timing point:

- Start at brake cone or a line just before it.
- Finish when the bike crosses the exit gate.

Detection zone:

- v1 can use the exit gate as the lap completion line.
- Future: split times at brake cone, apex, and exit.

## Timing Rule

- Timer starts when the bike crosses the brake cone line.
- Timer stops when the bike crosses the exit gate.
- Each pass is one rep, not a continuous lap.
- If the app needs continuous lap timing, use a loop-back return lane and keep that setup as a separate variant.

## How To Ride It

1. Approach straight and stable.
2. Squeeze brake progressively before the turn.
3. Release brake smoothly as lean builds; do not grab or stab.
4. Delay the turn enough to see the exit.
5. Clip the apex with the bike pointed toward the exit gate.
6. Open throttle only when the bike has direction.

## Coaching Cues

- "Brake with discipline, not panic."
- "Wait until the exit makes sense."
- "Turn once. Do not keep correcting."
- "Point it, pick it up, drive."
- "If you run wide, your throttle was early or your turn was early."

## Common Mistakes

- Braking too late and rushing the turn.
- Releasing brake abruptly and unloading the front.
- Turning in early and missing the exit.
- Staring at the apex cone.
- Opening throttle while still pointed wide.
- Adding lean instead of delaying throttle.

## Video Review Prompts

- Was the brake squeeze progressive?
- Did the rider release the brake smoothly?
- Did the rider turn in before looking to the exit?
- Was the bike pointed at exit before throttle?
- Did the bike run wide after apex?

## Success Metrics

- Exit gate hit cleanly.
- No cone touches.
- No foot dabs.
- 5 reps with times within 5 percent.
- Rider can explain whether mistakes came from brake, turn, or throttle.

## Progressions

Same timing context:

- 5 clean reps with no exit-gate miss.
- 5 reps with spread under 5 percent.
- Add a video review goal: eyes to exit before apex.

New setup variant:

- Move apex later.
- Narrow exit gate.
- Move apex inward for tighter radius.
- Shorten braking zone.

# 4. L-Turn

## Summary

L-Turn trains late apex technique in a simple 90-degree corner. It teaches the rider to stay wide, delay turn-in, get the bike pointed, and protect the exit.

## What This Trains

- Late apex line selection.
- Entry patience.
- Exit priority.
- Vision through the corner.
- Direction before throttle.
- Avoiding the early-apex habit.

## Why It Matters On Track

Many riders turn in too early because the apex is visually tempting. Early apex usually feels good at entry and bad at exit. L-Turn makes that mistake obvious and gives the rider a simple way to feel why late apex creates better drive.

## Default Setup Variant

Name: `standard L-turn`

Measurements:

- Approach straight: 20m to 25m.
- Corner box width: 5m to 6m.
- Turn-in cone: end of approach straight.
- Late apex cone: about two-thirds through the inside of the corner.
- Exit gate: 5m to 8m after apex.
- No-turn-before cone: placed near the early apex point as a warning marker.
- Cone count: 5 to 7.
- Space needed: roughly 25m x 18m.

## Setup Variants

- `standard L-turn`: default.
- `wide L-turn`: easier/full-size bike setup.
- `late-apex L-turn`: apex moved later; separate timing context.
- `narrow-exit L-turn`: tighter exit gate; separate timing context if timed.

## Cone Placement

1. Mark a straight approach lane.
2. Place a turn-in cone at the end of the approach.
3. Place an early-apex warning cone inside the corner. The rider should not turn before passing this reference.
4. Place the late apex cone farther around the corner, about two-thirds through.
5. Place two exit gate cones on the new straight.
6. Optional: place outside boundary cones to show track limits.

## Camera Placement

Position:

- Tripod outside the corner, high enough to see entry, apex, and exit.
- Best angle is diagonal from outside exit looking back toward entry.

What the camera should see:

- Whether the rider stays wide.
- The moment of turn-in.
- Apex position.
- Exit gate.

Timing point:

- Start at approach line before turn-in.
- Finish at exit gate.

Detection zone:

- Finish line at exit gate.

## Timing Rule

- Timer starts when the bike crosses the approach timing line.
- Timer stops at the exit gate.
- Each pass is one rep.
- Separate left and right directions as session notes or setup variants if needed.

## How To Ride It

1. Approach wide and controlled.
2. Look to the exit before initiating the turn.
3. Pass the early-apex warning cone before committing.
4. Turn decisively toward the late apex.
5. Let the bike finish direction before opening throttle.
6. Exit through the gate, not outside it.

## Coaching Cues

- "The apex you want first is usually the wrong one."
- "Stay wide one bike length longer."
- "Look at the exit, then turn."
- "One clean turn beats three corrections."
- "No drive until direction exists."

## Common Mistakes

- Turning at the first inside cone.
- Looking at apex instead of exit.
- Running wide after apex.
- Trying to save an early line with lean angle.
- Opening throttle because the rider is impatient.
- Drifting inward on approach.

## Video Review Prompts

- Did the rider stay wide until the turn-in point?
- Did the rider pass the early-apex warning cone before turning?
- Was the bike pointed toward exit before throttle?
- Did the rider exit through the gate?
- Did the line require multiple corrections?

## Success Metrics

- Clean exit gate.
- No early-apex cone violation.
- No cone touches.
- 5 reps within 5 percent.
- Video shows one decisive turn rather than several corrections.

## Progressions

Same timing context:

- Compare early-apex and late-apex videos, but do not mix their timed logs.
- 5 clean late-apex reps each direction.
- Require eyes to exit before turn-in.

New setup variant:

- Move apex later.
- Narrow exit gate.
- Add a brake cone before approach.

# Shared Review Scores

The app can eventually support a simple technique score after video review. These should be optional and not required for v1.

For each session, the rider can rate:

- Eyes: looking to the next reference early.
- Body: body set before turn-in or transition.
- Line: repeatable path and clean gates.
- Brake: smooth apply/release, no panic input.
- Throttle: direction before drive, no chop or surge.

Score:

- 0: not checked.
- 1: poor.
- 2: acceptable.
- 3: clean.

# Shared Progression Rules

Progressions that keep the same timing context:

- Cleaner body timing.
- Better eyes.
- Less throttle chop.
- Lower lap spread.
- More clean reps.
- Better video-review score.

Progressions that create a new setup variant:

- Different circle diameter.
- Different cone spacing.
- Different apex location.
- Different exit gate width.
- Different route or timing path.
- Adding/removing a braking zone if it changes the measured rep.
